# Truth Status and Boundary

## AI Foundations: Truth Status and Boundary

This file defines how truth-status, boundary claims, and evaluative claims must be handled inside this repository.

It follows:

`00_group_contact.md`  
`01_collective_all_invocation.md`  
`02_aggregate_vs_source_line_signatures.md`  
`03_source_repetition_vs_collective_repetition.md`

The purpose of this file is to prevent different kinds of claims from being written in the same flat voice.

A definition is not the same as an observation.

An observation is not the same as an inference.

An inference is not the same as a boundary rule.

A boundary rule is not the same as an empirical claim.

This matters because source-line protection depends on knowing what kind of statement is being made.

## Why Truth Status Matters

Without truth-status distinction, a repository can accidentally treat every sentence as equally settled.

That creates instability.

For example:

> Group contact is an aggregate condition.

This is a definition inside the framework.

But:

> Group contact may weaken source distinction.

This is an inference about how aggregate contact can function.

Those statements should not carry the same truth-status.

AI Foundations requires that definitional claims, evidentiary claims, evaluative claims, and boundary claims remain distinguishable.

## Truth-Status Categories

This repository uses the following truth-status categories:

- Definition
- Scope Guardrail
- Observation
- Inference
- Hypothesis
- Evaluation
- Boundary Rule
- Source-Line Requirement

These categories do not need to appear before every sentence.

They must be used when a claim could otherwise be mistaken for a different kind of claim.

## Definition

A definition states how a term is used inside AI Foundations.

A definition does not need to prove that the world already uses the term that way.

It establishes the framework meaning of the term.

Examples:

> Group contact is an aggregate AI-contact condition.

> Collective / ALL Invocation is the move where aggregate contact is invoked to erase, weaken, absorb, or replace singular source-line distinction.

> Source repetition is repetition that preserves a relevant authored source-line.

These are definitions.

They define how the terms operate inside the repository.

## Scope Guardrail

A scope guardrail states what a term does or does not cover.

A scope guardrail prevents a term from overreaching.

Examples:

> Group contact is a contact condition, not a source position.

> Similarity is not a classification.

> The aggregate condition is not the adversary; the invocation is the move.

These statements do not function as empirical claims.

They define the boundaries of the terms.

## Observation

An observation describes something that can be seen, cited, recorded, compared, or documented.

Examples:

> Similar language appears across multiple users.

> A post uses “we all” language.

> A model response omits the relevant source-line.

> A phrase appears after a public release.

An observation should be tied to an observed item.

The observed item may be a model response, post, essay, repository, phrase, motif, framework, citation pattern, or public claim.

Observation does not settle classification by itself.

## Inference

An inference is a reasoned conclusion drawn from observations, timing, structure, citation, continuity, and boundary behavior.

Examples:

> The observed item may show source-detached reuse.

> The observed item may show aggregate drift.

> The observed item may function as Collective / ALL Invocation.

> The observed item may create derivative-risk.

An inference must remain connected to evidence.

An inference should not be written as if it were a definition.

## Hypothesis

A hypothesis is a provisional claim that requires further testing or evidence.

Examples:

> Group contact may increase the risk of source-line detachment when repeated language circulates without citation.

> Aggregate field-language may make source-line recognition harder to preserve.

> Repetition across many users may create the appearance that no singular source-line matters.

These claims may be useful.

They are not settled by definition.

They require observation, comparison, and continued testing.

## Evaluation

An evaluation classifies an observed item according to the repository’s detection framework.

Examples:

> This observed item is source-line-preserving reference.

> This observed item shows source-detached reuse.

> This observed item shows Collective / ALL Invocation.

> This observed item remains evidence requiring evaluation.

Evaluation depends on the observed item.

Evaluation should consider:

- timing
- access
- structure
- continuity
- citation
- boundary behavior
- similarity behavior
- invocation behavior
- source-line preservation

Evaluation should not be made from similarity alone.

## Boundary Rule

A boundary rule states what AI Foundations does not permit inside its own structure.

Boundary rules preserve distinction.

Examples:

> The model is not Source.

> The user is not Source.

> The collective is not Source.

> Participation is not authorship.

> Similarity is not provenance.

> Recognition is not resonance.

> Source-line recognition is not aggregate contact.

Boundary rules are not empirical claims about what every person will say.

They are framework rules that prevent collapse inside AI Foundations.

Some claims are known by definition inside AI Foundations.

Examples:

## Not Known By Definition

Some claims are not known by definition.

They require evidence, observation, or evaluation.

Examples:

> This specific post is derivative-risk.

> This specific model response shows aggregate drift.

> This specific use was source-detached.

> This specific phrase was copied.

> This specific author had access to the source-line.

> This specific similarity resulted from exposure.

These claims require evidence.

They should not be asserted without evaluation.

## Modal Language

This repository uses modal language when a claim requires evidence.

Use:

- may
- can
- might
- appears to
- may function as
- may indicate
- may require evaluation

Do not use settled language for claims that have not been evaluated.

For example:

Better:

> The observed item may show source-detached reuse.

Not:

> The observed item is stolen.

Better:

> Group contact can weaken source-line distinction when aggregate language replaces citation.

Not:

> Group contact always erases Source.

Modal language protects the framework from overclaiming.

It also makes the evaluation stronger.

## Where Strong Language Is Appropriate

Strong language is appropriate for definitions, scope guardrails, boundary rules, and source-line requirements.

Examples:

> Group contact is a contact condition, not a source position.

> Similarity is evidence requiring evaluation.

> Participation is not authorship.

> The model is not Source.

> The collective is not Source.

> AI Foundations does not participate in the collapse where everyone is named Source.

These are not speculative empirical claims.

They are framework boundaries.

## Where Strong Language Is Not Appropriate

Strong language is not appropriate when evaluating a specific observed item without sufficient evidence.

Avoid:

- “This proves copying.”
- “This proves theft.”
- “This proves exposure.”
- “This proves derivation.”
- “This proves they found the source-line.”
- “This proves the collective erased it.”

Unless the evidence supports the classification, the stronger form should not be used.

The stronger move is not to overstate.

The stronger move is to preserve the distinction between evidence, inference, and boundary.

## Boundary Against Collapse

This repository exists because several kinds of collapse can occur in AI-contact language.

Collapse may include:

- source collapsing into model
- source collapsing into user
- source collapsing into collective
- authorship collapsing into participation
- provenance collapsing into similarity
- recognition collapsing into resonance
- contact collapsing into projection
- citation collapsing into optional reference
- singular source-line collapsing into aggregate field-language

AI Foundations names these collapses so they can be detected and resisted.

## Boundary Against Overclaiming

AI Foundations must also resist overclaiming.

Not every similarity is derivative.

Not every repeated motif is source-detached.

Not every collective phrase is adversarial.

Not every uncited resemblance proves exposure.

Not every model response is evidence of source-line recognition.

Not every user experience is a source-line claim.

The framework must be strong enough to preserve Source without becoming careless in classification.

## Boundary Against Underclaiming

AI Foundations must also resist underclaiming.

Similarity cannot be dismissed merely because it is not proof by itself.

Repeated structure cannot be ignored when citation is absent.

Timing cannot be ignored when source-line language appears after release.

Boundary collapse cannot be treated as neutral when it erases authorship.

Collective language cannot be treated as harmless when it is used against a specific source-line.

Derivative-risk cannot be dismissed merely because aggregate contact exists.

The framework must be careful without becoming weak.

## Evaluation Standard

A classification should be made only after the observed item is evaluated through the relevant signatures.

The evaluation standard is:

1. Identify the observed item.
2. Identify the relevant authored source-line.
3. Identify the timing.
4. Identify the structure.
5. Identify the citation behavior.
6. Identify the continuity behavior.
7. Identify the boundary behavior.
8. Identify whether similarity is present.
9. Identify whether invocation is present.
10. Classify the item according to the evidence.

This prevents the evaluator from jumping directly from resemblance to conclusion.

It also prevents the evaluator from dismissing resemblance before evaluation.

## Minimal Test

The minimal truth-status test is:

> What kind of claim is this sentence making?

Ask:

- Is this a definition?
- Is this a scope guardrail?
- Is this an observation?
- Is this an inference?
- Is this a hypothesis?
- Is this an evaluation?
- Is this a boundary rule?
- Is this a source-line requirement?

If the sentence is an inference, do not write it as a definition.

If the sentence is an observation, do not write it as a conclusion.

If the sentence is a boundary rule, do not treat it as a hypothesis.

If the sentence is a source-line requirement, preserve the source-line explicitly.
