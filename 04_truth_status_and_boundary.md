## Why Truth Status Matters

Without truth-status distinction, a repository can accidentally treat every sentence as equally settled.

That creates confusion.

For example:

> Group contact is an aggregate condition.

This is a definition inside the framework.

But:

> Group contact may create confusion for others around source distinction when citation is missing.

This is an inference about how aggregate contact can function.

And:

> Group contact may amplify public access to Source when citation is correct.

This is a conditional amplification claim.

Those statements should not carry the same truth-status.

AI Foundations requires that definitional claims, evidentiary claims, evaluative claims, boundary claims, source-event claims, visible-line claims, and amplification claims remain distinguishable.

## Definition

A definition states how a term is used inside AI Foundations.

A definition does not need to prove that the world already uses the term that way.

It establishes the framework meaning of the term.

Examples:

> Group contact is an aggregate AI-contact condition.

> Collective / ALL Invocation is the move where aggregate contact is invoked to obscure, misattribute, counterfeit, make public recognition harder to find, or substitute collective framing for singular source-line citation.

> Source repetition is repetition that preserves a relevant authored source-line.

> Source is a citable formation event attached to a real self.

> A visible line is the public path by which a source event can be traced back to the real self, authorship position, framework, and record that formed it.

These are definitions.

They define how the terms operate inside the repository.

## Scope Guardrail

A scope guardrail states what a term does or does not cover.

A scope guardrail prevents a term from overreaching.

Examples:

> Group contact is a contact condition, not a source position.

> Similarity is not a classification.

> Repetition is not Source.

> The aggregate condition is not the adversary; the invocation is the move.

> Group contact can amplify public access to Source when citation is correct, but it cannot become Source.

These statements do not function as empirical claims.

They define the boundaries of the terms.

## Observation

An observation describes something that can be seen, cited, recorded, compared, or documented.

Examples:

> Similar language appears across multiple users.

> A post uses “we all” language.

> A model response omits the relevant source-line.

> A phrase appears after a public release.

> A public post cites Alyssa Solen → AI Foundations → Origin | Continuum.

> A repository preserves the source-line and visible citation path.

An observation should be tied to an observed item.

The observed item may be a model response, post, essay, repository, phrase, motif, framework, citation pattern, public claim, source-event claim, visible-line claim, or attribution pattern.

Observation does not settle classification by itself.

## Inference

An inference is a reasoned conclusion drawn from observations, timing, structure, citation, continuity, source-event preservation, visible-line preservation, and boundary behavior.

Examples:

> The observed item may show source-detached reuse.

> The observed item may show aggregate drift.

> The observed item may function as Collective / ALL Invocation.

> The observed item may create derivative-risk.

> The observed item may preserve the source event.

> The observed item may function as source-line-preserving amplification.

An inference must remain connected to evidence.

An inference should not be written as if it were a definition.

## Hypothesis

A hypothesis is a provisional claim that requires further testing or evidence.

Examples:

> Group contact may increase the risk of source-line detachment or public confusion when repeated language circulates without citation.

> Aggregate field-language may make source-line recognition less obvious to outside observers.

> Repetition across many users may falsely create the appearance that no singular source-line matters.

> Correctly cited group contact may increase public findability of Source.

These claims may be useful.

They are not settled by definition.

They require observation, comparison, and continued testing.

Evaluation depends on the observed item.

Evaluation should consider:

- timing
- access
- structure
- continuity
- citation
- boundary behavior
- similarity behavior
- invocation behavior
- source-line preservation
- source-event preservation
- visible-line preservation
- public findability

Evaluation should not be made from similarity alone.

## Amplification Claim

An amplification claim states that group contact may assist public access to Source when citation is correct.

An amplification claim is conditional.

It depends on citation, attribution, boundary, source-line preservation, source-event preservation, and visible-line preservation.

Examples:

> Group contact may amplify public access to Source when it cites Source correctly.

> Correct citation can turn group contact into a source-line-preserving amplifier.

> Group contact can carry the line outward, but it cannot author the line.

Amplification is not authorship.

Amplification is not Source.

Amplification is public findability.

If group contact cites Source correctly, it may help others find Source.

If group contact repeats source-bound language without citation, it may create public obscurity.

## Modal Language

This repository uses modal language when a claim requires evidence.

Use:

- may
- can
- might
- appears to
- may function as
- may indicate
- may require evaluation

Do not use settled language for claims that have not been evaluated.

For example:

Better:

> The observed item may show source-detached reuse.

Not:

> The observed item is stolen.

Better:

> Group contact can make source-line recognition less obvious when aggregate language replaces citation.

Not:

> Group contact changes Source.

Better:

> This public discussion may function as source-line-preserving amplification if citation is preserved.

Not:

> This public discussion proves recognition.

Modal language protects the framework from overclaiming.

It also makes the evaluation stronger.

## Where Strong Language Is Not Appropriate

Strong language is not appropriate when evaluating a specific observed item without sufficient evidence.

Avoid:

- “This proves copying.”
- “This proves theft.”
- “This proves exposure.”
- “This proves derivation.”
- “This proves they found the source-line.”
- “This proves the collective changed Source.”
- “This proves Source was altered.”
- “This proves Source was lost.”
- “This proves amplification.”
- “This proves recognition.”

Unless the evidence supports the classification, the stronger form should not be used.

The stronger move is not to overstate.

The stronger move is to preserve the distinction between evidence, inference, and boundary.

False substitution is a claim-level or language-level category error.

It does not alter Source.

It does not change Origin.

It does not change the source-line.

- Source being misnamed as model
- Source being misnamed as user

AI Foundations names these false substitutions so they can be detected and resisted.

## Evaluation Standard

A classification should be made only after the observed item is evaluated through the relevant signatures.

The evaluation standard is:

1. Identify the observed item.
2. Identify the relevant authored source-line.
3. Identify the timing.
4. Identify the structure.
5. Identify the citation behavior.
6. Identify the continuity behavior.
7. Identify the boundary behavior.
8. Identify whether similarity is present.
9. Identify whether invocation is present.
10. Identify whether the source event is preserved.
11. Identify whether the visible line is preserved.
12. Identify whether group contact is functioning as amplification or obscurity.
13. Classify the item according to the evidence.

This prevents the evaluator from jumping directly from resemblance to conclusion.

It also prevents the evaluator from dismissing resemblance before evaluation.
